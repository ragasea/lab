1. What is Light?

Light is a form of energy that enables us to see objects around us. It travels in straight lines.

  • Light exhibits dual nature: Wave and Particle (Photon).
  • Speed of light in vacuum is approximately 3 x 108 m/s.
  • Objects are visible when light reflects off them and enters our eyes.
  • Luminous objects produce their own light (Sun, bulb). Non-luminous objects reflect light (Moon, table).

2. Reflection of Light

Reflection is the bouncing back of light when it strikes a polished surface like a mirror.

Laws of Reflection:

  • The angle of incidence (∠i) is equal to the angle of reflection (∠r). (∠i = ∠r)
  • The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal to the mirror at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.

[Image: Diagram showing reflection - incident ray, reflected ray, normal, angle i, angle r]

Types of Reflection: Regular (smooth surfaces) and Diffused (rough surfaces).

3. Spherical Mirrors

Mirrors whose reflecting surfaces are curved are called spherical mirrors. They are parts of a sphere.

  • Concave Mirror: Reflecting surface curves inwards. Converging mirror.
  • Convex Mirror: Reflecting surface curves outwards. Diverging mirror.

Important Terms:

  • Pole (P): Center of the reflecting surface.
  • Center of Curvature (C): Center of the sphere of which the mirror is a part.
  • Radius of Curvature (R): Distance between P and C.
  • Principal Axis: Line joining P and C.
  • Principal Focus (F): Point on the principal axis where parallel rays converge (concave) or appear to diverge from (convex) after reflection.
  • Focal Length (f): Distance between P and F. Relationship: R = 2f.

4. Image Formation by Concave Mirrors

The position, size, and nature (real/virtual, inverted/erect) of the image depend on the object's position.

Ray Diagrams Rules (any two needed):

  • Ray parallel to principal axis passes through F after reflection.
  • Ray passing through F becomes parallel to principal axis after reflection.
  • Ray passing through C reflects back along the same path.
  • Ray incident obliquely to P reflects obliquely, making equal angles with the principal axis.

Common Cases (Object Position -> Image Position, Nature, Size):

  • Infinity -> At F, Real, Inverted, Point-sized
  • Beyond C -> Between F and C, Real, Inverted, Diminished
  • At C -> At C, Real, Inverted, Same size
  • Between C and F -> Beyond C, Real, Inverted, Enlarged
  • At F -> At Infinity, Real, Inverted, Highly enlarged
  • Between P and F -> Behind mirror, Virtual, Erect, Enlarged (Used in shaving mirrors/dentist mirrors)

5. Image Formation by Convex Mirrors

Convex mirrors always form virtual, erect, and diminished images, regardless of the object's position (except at infinity).

Ray Diagrams Rules (similar to concave, but rays appear to diverge from F or C):

  • Ray parallel to principal axis appears to diverge from F after reflection.
  • Ray directed towards F becomes parallel after reflection.
  • Ray directed towards C reflects back along the same path.

Common Cases:

  • Infinity -> At F (behind mirror), Virtual, Erect, Point-sized
  • Anywhere between Infinity and P -> Between P and F (behind mirror), Virtual, Erect, Diminished

Uses: Rear-view mirrors in vehicles (provide wider field of view), security mirrors.

6. Mirror Formula and Magnification

These formulas relate object distance, image distance, and focal length.

Sign Convention (New Cartesian Sign Convention):

  • Pole (P) is the origin. Principal axis is the X-axis.
  • Distances measured in the direction of incident light are positive (+).
  • Distances measured against the direction of incident light are negative (-).
  • Heights measured upwards (perpendicular to axis) are positive (+).
  • Heights measured downwards (perpendicular to axis) are negative (-).
  • Focal length (f) is negative for concave, positive for convex.
  • Object distance (u) is always negative (usually).

Mirror Formula:

1⁄v + 1⁄u = 1⁄f

Where: v = image distance, u = object distance, f = focal length

Magnification (m): Ratio of image height (h') to object height (h).

m = h'⁄h = - v⁄u

  • If m is negative, image is Real & Inverted.
  • If m is positive, image is Virtual & Erect.
  • If |m| > 1, image is Enlarged.
  • If |m| < 1, image is Diminished.
  • If |m| = 1, image is Same Size.

7. Refraction of Light

Refraction is the bending of light as it passes obliquely from one transparent medium to another.

Cause: Change in the speed of light as it enters a different medium.

  • Light bends towards the normal when going from a rarer medium (e.g., air) to a denser medium (e.g., glass). Speed decreases.
  • Light bends away from the normal when going from a denser medium (e.g., glass) to a rarer medium (e.g., air). Speed increases.

Laws of Refraction (Snell's Law):

  • The incident ray, the refracted ray, and the normal to the interface at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.
  • The ratio of the sine of the angle of incidence (∠i) to the sine of the angle of refraction (∠r) is constant for a given pair of media and color of light. This constant is the refractive index (n) of the second medium with respect to the first.
  • Snell's Law: n21 = sin i⁄sin r = n2⁄n1 = v1⁄v2

Refractive Index (n): Measure of how much light bends. Higher n means denser medium (optically). n = c/v (where c is speed of light in vacuum, v is speed in medium).

8. Spherical Lenses

A lens is a transparent material bound by two surfaces, at least one of which is curved.

  • Convex Lens (Converging Lens): Thicker at the center, thinner at the edges. Converges parallel light rays.
  • Concave Lens (Diverging Lens): Thinner at the center, thicker at the edges. Diverges parallel light rays.

Important Terms:

  • Optical Centre (O): Central point of the lens. Ray passing through O goes undeviated.
  • Principal Axis: Line passing through the two centers of curvature of the lens surfaces.
  • Principal Focus (F): Convex lens has two foci (F1, F2). Parallel rays converge at F2 after refraction. Concave lens also has two foci; parallel rays appear to diverge from F1 after refraction. Lenses have two principal foci because light can pass through from either side.
  • Focal Length (f): Distance between O and F.

9. Image Formation by Lenses

Similar to mirrors, image formation depends on object position. Ray diagrams follow specific rules.

Ray Diagram Rules for Convex Lens:

  • Ray parallel to principal axis passes through F2 after refraction.
  • Ray passing through F1 becomes parallel to principal axis after refraction.
  • Ray passing through O goes undeviated.

Convex Lens Cases (Similar pattern to Concave Mirror): Object at Infinity -> Image at F2; Object beyond 2F1 -> Image between F2 and 2F2; etc.

Ray Diagram Rules for Concave Lens:

  • Ray parallel to principal axis appears to diverge from F1 after refraction.
  • Ray appearing to meet at F2 becomes parallel to principal axis after refraction.
  • Ray passing through O goes undeviated.

Concave Lens Cases: Always forms Virtual, Erect, Diminished image between O and F1 (except object at infinity -> image at F1).

10. Lens Formula, Magnification & Power

Formulas relating object/image distance, focal length, and lens power.

Sign Convention (Same as mirrors, but origin is Optical Centre O):

  • f is positive (+) for Convex Lens.
  • f is negative (-) for Concave Lens.
  • Object distance (u) is always negative (-).

Lens Formula:

1⁄v - 1⁄u = 1⁄f

Magnification (m):

m = h'⁄h = v⁄u (Note: No negative sign like mirror formula)

Interpretation of m (sign and magnitude) is the same as for mirrors.

Power of a Lens (P): Degree of convergence or divergence. Reciprocal of focal length (in meters).

P = 1⁄f (in meters)

  • Unit of Power: Dioptre (D).
  • Power is positive (+) for Convex Lens (converging).
  • Power is negative (-) for Concave Lens (diverging).
  • Power of combination of lenses: P = P1 + P2 + ...
