1. Reflection of Light

Reflection is the bouncing back of light when it strikes a smooth surface, like a mirror. The two laws of reflection are:

  • The angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection.
  • The incident ray, reflected ray, and the normal at the point of incidence lie in the same plane.

Types of reflection include regular reflection (on smooth surfaces) and diffuse reflection (on rough surfaces).

2. Spherical Mirrors

Spherical mirrors are curved mirrors, either concave (curving inward) or convex (curving outward). Key terms include:

  • Pole: The center of the mirror.
  • Focus: The point where light rays converge or appear to diverge.
  • Radius of Curvature: Twice the focal length.

Concave mirrors can form real or virtual images, while convex mirrors always form virtual images.

3. Refraction of Light

Refraction is the bending of light as it passes from one medium to another due to a change in speed. The laws of refraction are:

  • The incident ray, refracted ray, and normal lie in the same plane.
  • Snell's Law: The ratio of the sine of the angle of incidence to the sine of the angle of refraction is constant.

Refraction causes objects to appear at different positions, like a pencil in water appearing bent.

4. Lenses

Lenses are transparent objects that refract light to form images. There are two types:

  • Convex Lens: Converges light rays, can form real or virtual images.
  • Concave Lens: Diverges light rays, forms virtual images.

The power of a lens is measured in diopters, where Power = 1/focal length (in meters).

5. Dispersion and Spectrum

Dispersion is the splitting of white light into its constituent colors (VIBGYOR) when it passes through a prism. This happens because different colors of light refract by different amounts.

A spectrum is the band of colors formed due to dispersion. Rainbows are natural examples of spectra caused by water droplets acting as prisms.
